Security Tips

How to Stay Secure Online

A strong password is your first line of defense. Here's how to protect yourself.

🎲

Use Random Passwords

Never use dictionary words, names, or predictable patterns. A truly random 16-character password is trillions of times harder to crack than "Password123!".

🔁

Unique Password Per Account

If one site is breached and you reuse passwords, all your accounts become vulnerable. Each account needs its own unique password — a password manager makes this easy.

📏

Longer Is Exponentially Stronger

Every extra character multiplies the cracking time. Going from 10 to 16 characters can increase brute-force time from minutes to billions of years.

🗄️

Use a Password Manager

Tools like Bitwarden (free), 1Password, or Dashlane store all your passwords securely. You only need to remember one strong master password.

🛡️

Enable Two-Factor Auth

Add 2FA to every critical account. Even if your password is compromised, the attacker still can't log in without your second factor (phone or authenticator app).

🔔

Check for Data Breaches

Visit HaveIBeenPwned.com to check if your email or passwords have appeared in known data breaches. Change compromised passwords immediately.

Security experts recommend passwords of at least 12 characters, but 16 or more is ideal for critical accounts like banking or email. Longer passwords are exponentially harder to crack — a 16-character random password would take billions of years to brute-force with current technology. For less critical accounts, 12 characters is usually fine.
A strong password combines four qualities: (1) Length — at least 12 characters, ideally 16+; (2) Complexity — mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols; (3) Randomness — not based on dictionary words, names, or personal info; (4) Uniqueness — never reused across multiple accounts. Our generator meets all four criteria by design.

Entropy measures password unpredictability in bits. It's calculated as: length × log₂(charset size). For example, a 16-character password using all character types (94-char set) has 16 × log₂(94) ≈ 104 bits of entropy. Each additional bit doubles the number of guesses needed. 60+ bits is considered good; 80+ bits is excellent; 100+ bits is practically uncrackable by any known method.
Modern NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) guidance says you don't need to change passwords on a regular schedule unless there's a specific reason — like a known data breach, suspicion of compromise, or sharing access that needs to be revoked. The priority should be using strong, unique passwords everywhere, not frequently rotating weak ones. Do check HaveIBeenPwned.com periodically to catch any breaches.
