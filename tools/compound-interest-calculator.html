The Basics

Why Compound Interest Matters

Understanding the difference between simple and compound interest can change how you invest forever.

💸
Simple Interest
Interest is calculated only on the original principal amount throughout the investment period. The interest earned each year stays the same and does not get reinvested. It grows in a straight line.
₹1L at 10% for 10 yrs → ₹2,00,000 (Simple)
🚀
Compound Interest
Interest is calculated on the principal PLUS all previously earned interest. The interest itself earns interest, creating an exponential snowball effect. The longer you stay invested, the faster it grows.
₹1L at 10% for 10 yrs → ₹2,59,374 (Compound)
📅
Compounding Frequency
The more frequently interest is compounded, the more you earn. Monthly compounding yields more than quarterly, which yields more than yearly. Indian FDs typically use quarterly compounding; mutual funds use daily NAV calculation.
Monthly vs Yearly: +₹3,000–₹50,000 extra on ₹10L over 10 yrs
⏳
Time is the Superpower
The biggest factor in compounding is time. Starting 10 years earlier can double your final corpus. This is the reason financial advisors say "invest early, invest consistently" — the Rule of 72 tells you how long to double your money.
Rule of 72: Years to double = 72 ÷ Interest Rate
The Math

Compound Interest Formula

The standard formula used by all banks, NBFCs, and investment platforms in India and worldwide.

A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t)

Compound Interest = A − P
A
Final amount (Future Value) — the total money at the end of the investment period
P
Principal — the initial amount you invest or deposit
r
Annual interest rate as a decimal (e.g. 8% = 0.08)
n
Number of compounding periods per year (monthly = 12, quarterly = 4, yearly = 1)
t
Time in years — the duration of your investment
CI
Compound Interest earned = A − P (total earnings above your principal)

Smart Tips

Maximize Your Compound Interest

Proven strategies to make compounding work harder for you.

⏰

Start as Early as Possible

Starting just 5 years earlier can increase your final corpus by 50–100%. Time is the most powerful variable in compounding.

🔄

Reinvest All Returns

Never withdraw interest mid-way. Every rupee reinvested works harder. Choose growth options in mutual funds over dividend payout.

📅

Prefer Monthly Compounding

When choosing between FDs or savings plans with different compounding frequencies, always pick the most frequent — monthly beats quarterly beats yearly.

💳

Use SIP for Regular Growth

A monthly SIP compounds every instalment individually. ₹5,000/month at 12% for 20 years grows to over ₹49L versus ₹12L invested.

🏦

Choose Tax-Efficient Accounts

PPF, ELSS, and NPS offer tax benefits that effectively boost your real return rate. Tax savings compound too!

📉

Avoid Premature Withdrawal

Breaking an FD or exiting a mutual fund early resets the compounding clock. Even a 2-year gap can cost lakhs in long-term wealth.

Got Questions?

Frequently Asked Questions

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and all previously accumulated interest. Unlike simple interest (which earns the same amount each year), compound interest grows exponentially. Albert Einstein reportedly called it the "eighth wonder of the world" — those who understand it earn it, and those who don't pay it.
The formula is A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t), where A is the final amount, P is the principal, r is the annual interest rate in decimal form, n is the number of compounding periods per year, and t is the time in years. The compound interest earned is simply A minus P. Our calculator applies this formula instantly with your inputs.
Simple interest is always calculated only on the original principal. Compound interest is calculated on the principal plus all accumulated interest. For example: ₹1 lakh at 10% for 10 years gives ₹2,00,000 (simple) vs ₹2,59,374 (compound monthly). The gap widens dramatically over longer periods — this is the power of compounding.
More frequent compounding always yields more. Daily > Monthly > Quarterly > Half-Yearly > Yearly. However, the practical difference between daily and monthly is very small. Most Indian banks offer quarterly compounding for FDs. Mutual fund NAVs are calculated daily, effectively giving daily compounding. For savings accounts, compounding is typically quarterly.
With a SIP, each monthly instalment is a new principal that starts compounding immediately. The first instalment compounds for the full tenure, the second for tenure minus one month, and so on. Over 20 years, even a ₹5,000/month SIP at 12% returns approximately ₹49.96 lakhs from just ₹12 lakh invested — thanks to compounding on each individual instalment.
The Rule of 72 is a quick mental calculation to estimate how long it takes for an investment to double at a given compound interest rate. Simply divide 72 by the annual interest rate. For example: at 8% interest, your money doubles in 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years. At 12%, it doubles in 6 years. It works best for rates between 6% and 20%.
