What is a Currency Converter?

A currency converter is an online tool that calculates the equivalent value of one currency in another using the current foreign exchange (forex) rate. Whether you're planning international travel, making cross-border payments, or monitoring global markets, a currency converter gives you instant, accurate conversions at your fingertips.

Our free online currency converter uses real-time mid-market exchange rates — the benchmark rate you see on financial news platforms like Reuters and Bloomberg. This rate is the midpoint between the global buy and sell prices for a given currency pair.

Supporting over 100 world currencies including major pairs like USD/INR, EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY, and exotic pairs, QuickTools Currency Converter is your go-to forex calculation tool.

How Currency Exchange Rates Work

Foreign exchange rates are determined by global currency markets (forex markets) which operate 24 hours a day, 5 days a week. Rates fluctuate based on economic indicators, central bank decisions, geopolitical events, trade flows, and investor sentiment.

There are three main types of exchange rates you'll encounter:

  • Mid-market rate — the fair, unbiased rate between buy and sell prices
  • Buy rate — the rate at which banks buy foreign currency from you
  • Sell rate — the rate at which banks sell foreign currency to you

Banks, money exchanges, and services like PayPal or Western Union add a markup to the mid-market rate, which is their margin. Our converter always shows the mid-market rate so you know the real value.

Currency Conversion Formula

The currency conversion formula is straightforward:

Converted Amount = Amount × Exchange Rate

Where Exchange Rate is the current value of the target currency relative to the source currency.

Cross Rate = Rate(A→USD) / Rate(B→USD)

For non-USD pairs, conversion happens via the USD as the common base — this is called a cross rate calculation. For example, to convert EUR to INR, the calculator uses EUR/USD and USD/INR rates and computes EUR/INR automatically.

✈️ Travel Currency Tips
Smart tips to get the best exchange rates when travelling internationally
1

Use Mid-Market Rate as Benchmark

Always check the mid-market rate before exchanging money. It's the fairest rate — any provider offering significantly less is taking a large margin.

2

Avoid Airport Currency Exchange

Airport exchange kiosks notoriously offer the worst rates. Exchange money beforehand or use ATMs at your destination instead.

3

Use Local ATMs Abroad

Withdrawing local cash from ATMs often gives you near mid-market rates. Look for ATMs from major local banks to avoid high fees.

4

Avoid Dynamic Currency Conversion

When paying abroad, always choose to pay in the local currency. "Pay in your home currency" options always use worse rates.

5

Watch for Hidden Fees

Banks and exchange services may charge flat fees per transaction. Factor these in when converting small amounts, as they can significantly impact the effective rate.

6

Monitor Rate Trends

If your travel is weeks away, monitor exchange rate trends. Converting at a favorable time can save a meaningful percentage on large amounts.

What is the difference between mid-market rate and bank rate?
+
The mid-market rate (also called interbank rate) is the midpoint between global buy and sell prices for a currency pair. It's the most accurate representation of a currency's true value. Banks, exchange services, and apps add a markup (0.5% to 5%) on top of this rate — that's how they earn revenue. Our converter always shows the mid-market rate so you have a fair reference point.
How often are exchange rates updated?
+
Exchange rates in global forex markets change every second during trading hours. Our tool fetches fresh rates when you load the page and when you perform a conversion, ensuring you always work with up-to-date data. For highly volatile markets or critical financial decisions, consult a professional forex broker.

What is a cross rate in currency conversion?
+
A cross rate is the exchange rate between two currencies, neither of which is the US Dollar. For example, the EUR/INR rate is a cross rate. It's typically calculated by dividing each currency's exchange rate against the USD. For instance: EUR/INR = (EUR/USD) × (USD/INR). Our converter handles all cross-rate calculations automatically.
