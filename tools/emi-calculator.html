Understanding EMI

How EMI is Calculated

The math behind your monthly loan payment, explained simply.

EMI (Equated Monthly Instalment) is computed using the standard reducing-balance formula used by all banks and NBFCs in India:

EMI = P × r × (1+r)ⁿ ÷ [(1+r)ⁿ – 1]
EEquated Monthly Instalment — the fixed amount paid each month
PPrincipal — the original loan amount borrowed
rMonthly interest rate = Annual rate ÷ 12 ÷ 100
nNumber of monthly instalments (loan tenure in months)
📉
Reducing Balance Method

Each month, interest is charged only on the outstanding principal — not the original loan. So your interest component decreases and principal repayment increases over time.

📅
Tenure Trade-off

Longer tenure = lower EMI, but you pay more total interest. Shorter tenure = higher EMI, but you save significantly on interest. Use the calculator to find your sweet spot.

💳
Prepayment Power

Even one lump-sum prepayment early in the loan cycle can dramatically reduce your total interest burden and shorten the loan tenure without changing your EMI.

📊
FOIR Rule of Thumb

Banks use FOIR (Fixed Obligation to Income Ratio). Keep all EMIs below 40–50% of your net monthly income for healthy loan eligibility and financial stability.

Got Questions?

Frequently Asked Questions

EMI (Equated Monthly Instalment) is the fixed amount you pay your lender every month until the loan is fully repaid. Each payment covers both the interest for that month and a portion of the principal. Early in the tenure, most of the EMI goes toward interest. Gradually, as the outstanding principal reduces, more of your EMI repays the principal — this is called the reducing balance method.
Home loan EMI uses the formula: EMI = P × r × (1+r)ⁿ ÷ [(1+r)ⁿ – 1]. For example, a ₹50 lakh home loan at 8.5% for 20 years gives a monthly rate r = 8.5/12/100 = 0.007083, n = 240 months, and EMI ≈ ₹43,391. Our calculator applies this exact formula with full precision.
Most financial planners recommend keeping your total monthly EMI obligations (all loans combined) below 40% of your net monthly take-home salary. Banks use a metric called FOIR (Fixed Obligation to Income Ratio) — typically they allow lending up to 50–55% FOIR for applicants with high income and strong credit profiles.
You can reduce your EMI by: (1) Making a larger down payment to reduce the principal; (2) Choosing a longer loan tenure; (3) Negotiating a lower interest rate — improve your CIBIL score above 750 to get the best rates; (4) Balance transferring your loan to a lender offering lower rates; (5) Making periodic part-prepayments to reduce the outstanding balance.
In a flat rate loan, interest is charged on the full original principal for the entire tenure — making the effective cost much higher than the stated rate. In a reducing balance loan (used by most banks in India), interest is calculated on the remaining outstanding principal each month. Our calculator uses the reducing balance method, which is the standard for home, car, and personal loans.
